Ground level — Foundation

0 – 15m · Where all strength begins

Level 1 · 5m

Dead hang

The foundation of everything overhead. Hang from a bar with full bodyweight — no kipping, no swinging. Builds grip, shoulder packing, and scapular awareness that every advanced skill depends on.

Most beginners can't hang for more than 20 seconds. Build to 60s before moving to any pulling work. Your connective tissue needs this adaptation more than your muscles do.

Level 2 · 10m

Pull-up

The benchmark of upper-body pulling strength. Chin over bar from a full dead hang, elbows completely extended at the bottom every rep. No momentum. If you can't do 10 strict reps, don't attempt anything above this line.

Elite gymnasts can perform 30+ strict pull-ups. At bodyweight, 20 clean reps puts you in the top 1% of the population. The world record is 612 in under an hour.

The skill zone — Intermediate

15 – 40m · Where most people quit

Level 3 · 20m

Muscle-up

The transition from pull to push. Generate enough force to pull to hip height, then shift your wrists over the bar into a dip. The strict version — no kip — demands explosive pulling power that takes months to develop.

The transition is the hardest part. Most people can pull and dip separately but fail the moment between. Fix: practice explosive chest-to-bar pull-ups until the bar touches your sternum on every rep.

Level 4 · 28m

Front lever

A horizontal iron cross on the bar. Body perfectly parallel to the ground, arms straight, core rigid. Every muscle from fingertips to toes under isometric tension simultaneously. Even a 2-second hold signals serious strength.

The front lever is a FIG gymnastics skill. It requires extraordinary lat activation — the lats must depress and retract the scapula while resisting shoulder flexion under full bodyweight. Most training books don't mention it until year three.

The elite zone — Advanced

40 – 80m · Fewer than 1 in 1,000 trainees reach this

Level 5 · 45m

Back lever

Body inverted and horizontal behind the bar, arms overhead in full extension. An underrated prerequisite for iron cross and planche work. Demands scapular stability in a position most bodies have never been asked to hold.

The back lever was once a required element in men's Olympic gymnastics. It was removed from the code of points in the 1990s — not because it became easy, but because the sport moved on to harder things above it.

Level 6 · 60m

Human flag

Body horizontal, perpendicular to a vertical pole. One arm pushes, one arm pulls, the torso stays rigid parallel to the ground. You are cantilevering your entire bodyweight off a single anchor point — pure physics.

The human flag generates no less than 5x bodyweight force through the shoulder joints. Six months of dedicated pole work is the realistic minimum for a first 2-second hold — starting from a strong pulling base.

The stratosphere — Expert

80 – 140m · Measured in years, not months

Level 7 · 90m

Handstand push-up

Inverted overhead pressing against gravity. The freestanding version requires a stable handstand, then strict vertical pressing through full range. The shoulder joint works in the exact opposite position from all natural human movement.

A freestanding handstand push-up requires solving two problems simultaneously: the balance of the handstand, and the strength of the press. Most people spend 6–12 months on each separately before combining them.

Level 8 · 110m

Handstand pull-up

Begin inverted. Pull. The bar ends up at your waist. Requires overhead pulling strength that barely exists in conventional training — resisting gravity in the direction it least expects through a full 180-degree range of motion.

Handstand pull-ups are rare even in professional gymnastics. The combination of freestanding stability and extreme inverted pulling strength puts this skill in a category that most lifetime athletes never reach.

The summit — Mastery

140m+ · Where physics becomes personal

Level 9 · 150m

Iron cross

Arms extended perfectly horizontal on the rings, body vertical. The shoulder joint is at maximum mechanical disadvantage — the lever arm is the entire arm length. Rings don't forgive. Every wobble is amplified. Gymnasts spend years for a 2-second hold.

Olympic gymnasts performing an iron cross generate forces of 7–10x bodyweight through the shoulder joint. The pectoralis minor and subscapularis contract isometrically at lengths they were never designed for. And they hold it for 2 full seconds.

Level 10 · 200m

Planche

Horizontal in the air, supported only by the hands, arms straight. No rings. No bar. Nothing but wrist strength, anterior deltoid capacity, and an iron core. The hardest static bodyweight skill that exists. Average training time to full planche: 3–5 years.

In a planche, your centre of mass must sit directly over your wrists — but your arms are angled backward. The anterior deltoids generate enormous force to prevent shoulder collapse. Wrist conditioning alone takes 6–12 months before full training loads can be applied.

You've reached the summit.

The planche sits at the top of the bodyweight skill tree. A handful of athletes in the world can hold it for more than 5 seconds. It takes years to build. Inches of progress feel like miles.

But it starts the same way every time. A bar. Your hands. A dead hang.
